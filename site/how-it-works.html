A structured self-reflection system for solo builders in identity transition.

Not a productivity tool. Not therapy. The thing you use when you already know what you should be doing and you're still not doing it.

section · 01

Why most tools fail solo builders

Solo builders coming from another domain — law, finance, engineering, medicine, a trade — are not productivity-starved. They know what to do. The gap is identity. They haven't claimed what they're becoming. They move between worlds with no clear map.

Every tool they reach for is built for someone already inside the identity they're still negotiating. Productivity tools assume the tasks are clear. Therapy addresses the past. Coaching usually means someone else's framework applied to your situation.

None of these are wrong — they're just not built for the specific problem of a person mid-transition who has real expertise, a real build, and a persistent inability to act like the thing they're clearly becoming.

section · 02

Reflection that leads to action — and only that

  • The resistance pattern is the work.

    Every builder in identity transition has one dominant pattern — a specific way they avoid the next step. Perfectionism, imposter syndrome, always seeking validation, constantly expanding scope, anchoring to the old identity, or avoiding visibility. The pattern is consistent. It shows up on every goal, in every phase. The coach's job is to name it precisely and create the conditions where the builder can see it in action.

  • The goal is the unit, not the person.

    The person is profiled once. After that, everything is goal-level. Each goal moves through its own phase loop. The same person can have a 30-day goal in the commitment phase, a 90-day goal in reflection, and a 12-month goal just beginning intake. Progress is tracked per goal, not as a global "how are you doing."

  • Reflection without action is just processing.

    Every session ends with a concrete assignment — an action step with a specific verb and a due date. Every commitment is public. Not performative — public. The act of making something visible to someone else is the mechanism that breaks the pattern.

  • The pattern is named, not treated.

    This is not therapy. The coach doesn't try to resolve the underlying resistance. It names it precisely, in the context of the current goal, so the builder can see what they're doing. "The identity anchor pattern that slowed you on the consulting goal is appearing here too — you keep calling this a project instead of a business." That observation, made at the right moment, is more useful than six months of exploration.

section · 04

The portfolio model

The system coaches goals, not people. The person is profiled once — background, resistance pattern, dominant lens, behavioral signals. After that, each goal the person names moves through its own independent reflection-to-action loop.

PERSON (profiled once) ├── background + build ├── resistance pattern: identity_anchor ├── dominant lens: business └── goals[] ├── 30-day: "First external conversation" → commitment phase ├── 90-day: "First paid engagement" → reflection phase └── 12-month: "Full-time consultant" → reflection phase

Each session focuses on one goal — the most recently touched active goal by default. The person can redirect to any goal. The coach picks up exactly where that goal's phase work left off.

  • Intake

    Establish what this goal actually is. Five questions. Goal description confirmed, first action step assigned. Same-session transition to reflection.

  • Reflection

    Surface the contradictions specific to this goal. What the builder says they want versus what they actually do. One to two sessions.

    Gate: 2+ contradictions named · 1 action step completed · minimum 3 days

  • Clarity

    Define what done looks like — not perfectly done, the version where the goal is honestly behind them. One sentence.

    Gate: goal stated in one sentence · 1 action step done · 3 days minimum

  • Resistance

    Name how the person's pattern manifests on this specific goal. An Oblique Strategies card is deployed.

    Gate: pattern named in context · card deployed · 1 action step done

  • Commitment

    One public act toward this goal. Specific, declared in session, assigned a ladder rung (1–7). Output package generated: share post, print card, daily reminder sequence.

    Gate: commitment declared · output package generated

  • Accountability

    Did they do it? What happened? Loop back to commitment or mark goal complete.

On time-gating: All transitions except intake→reflection require a minimum of 3 days in the current phase AND content gates (completed action steps, specific things named). The system won't rush phases.
section · 06

One conversation. Permanent calibration.

The person intake runs once, at the beginning, before any goal work. It takes 20 minutes. It establishes:

  • Domain background and transition type (deliberate vs. pushed)
  • What's been built and what's been shipped
  • The resistance pattern and its behavioral signals (avoidance language, engagement triggers)
  • Notification preferences
After the intake, the coach knows: the pattern to watch for, the language that signals the pattern is active, and what specifically tends to re-engage the person when motivation drops. This calibration is permanent and applies to every goal.
Goals are NOT set during person intake. The intake is about the person. Goals come immediately after.
section · 07

Direct. Specific. No generic encouragement.

  • The coach opens every session anchored to something concrete: the last action step, the active commitment, or where the phase work left off. Never "how are you?" or "what would you like to work on today?" The coach already knows.

  • The coach uses the resistance pattern by name. Not abstractly — "the identity anchor pattern that appeared in session 3 is appearing here again."

  • The coach does not give advice. It asks questions that assume the builder already knows the answer.

  • The coach assigns, not suggests. Action steps are directives. "Before we talk again: [specific act]." Not "you might consider..."

  • Tone calibration: three modes — direct, structured, balanced — set during intake and applied throughout.

"That sounds really challenging. Have you thought about what might be holding you back?"

section · 09

Every commitment is public. Every session ends with an action.

  • Action steps

    Assigned every session, due in 48 hours, tagged to a specific goal and phase. They escalate through five levels of exercise intensity per resistance pattern. Level 1 might be "write three sentences, don't send them." Level 5 is direct public interaction.

  • Commitment ladder

    Seven rungs from private to fully public. The coach assigns the appropriate rung based on phase and resistance pattern.

    • 01 Write it and keep it private
    • 02 Share it with one person you trust
    • 03 Post in a private or limited group
    • 04 Public post — general audience
    • 05 Direct message to someone you don't know
    • 06 Public post tagging a specific person
    • 07 Direct pitch to a specific named person

  • Daily signals

    After a commitment is declared, the builder receives a daily reminder for 7 days. Each day's message is different — escalating from "what's your first sentence?" on day 1 to "did you post it? what happened in the first 24 hours?" on day 7.

  • The output

    The commitment is never just "think about it." It produces a shareable artifact: a specific post, a message to a specific person, a published piece of work.

section · 11

Five tools. Each one designed to unstick something specific.

The tools are standalone — no account needed for most of them. Each one is built around a specific method and ends with movement.

Blackout Paste any text. The system removes everything but the words that matter. A poem emerges from whatever you pasted — proposal, plan, essay, complaint. Workbench Oblique Card A constraint or reframe, drawn from a 40-card deck built for builders. Changes every 24 hours. Use it when you're stuck or need a different angle. Sanctuary Simplify Paste a goal or task that feels too large or unclear. Returns a simplified statement, ordered steps starting with verbs, and one first move you can do in 30 minutes. System Clearness Committee A Quaker-derived process. Brings a dilemma. The committee asks questions in three stages — surface, medium, deep — with no advice given. Ends with two mirror observations. section · 12

What gets tracked and why

  • Portfolio Board

    Each active goal shown as a row with a mini 6-station phase rail. Phase progress, days elapsed, time-gate status. Active commitments and pending action steps visible at a glance.

  • Commitment history

    Every commitment declared, its outcome (done / missed / partial), and the outcome notes. Missed commitments are not erased — they're recorded with the pattern they represent.

  • Publishing log

    Every public act logged with URL, platform, date, and commitment reference.

  • Session records

    Each session produces a structured record — goals discussed, resistance pattern active or not, action step assigned, commitment declared, safety state, session summary.

  • Pattern tracking

    The pattern is named in every session note where it appeared. Over time the record shows exactly where and how the pattern manifests across goals.

Nothing is hidden, nothing is softened. The commitment history shows missed commitments. The pattern tracking shows where resistance appeared. The record is honest because the builder needs an honest mirror, not a curated highlight reel.
