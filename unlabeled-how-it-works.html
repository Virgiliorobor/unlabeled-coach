A structured self-reflection system for solo builders in identity transition.

Not a productivity tool. Not therapy. The thing you use when you already know what you should be doing and you're still not doing it.

01 — THE PROBLEM

Why most tools fail solo builders

Solo builders coming from another domain — law, finance, engineering, medicine, a trade — are not productivity-starved. They know what to do. The gap is identity. They haven't claimed what they're becoming. They move between worlds with no clear map.

Every tool they reach for is built for someone already inside the identity they're still negotiating. Productivity tools assume the tasks are clear. Therapy addresses the past. Coaching usually means someone else's framework applied to your situation.

None of these are wrong — they're just not built for the specific problem of a person mid-transition who has real expertise, a real build, and a persistent inability to act like the thing they're clearly becoming.

02 — PHILOSOPHY

Reflection that leads to action — and only that

The resistance pattern is the work.

Every builder in identity transition has one dominant pattern — a specific way they avoid the next step. Perfectionism, imposter syndrome, always seeking validation, constantly expanding scope, anchoring to the old identity, or avoiding visibility. The pattern is consistent. It shows up on every goal, in every phase. The coach's job is to name it precisely and create the conditions where the builder can see it in action.

The goal is the unit, not the person.

The person is profiled once. After that, everything is goal-level. Each goal moves through its own phase loop. The same person can have a 30-day goal in the commitment phase, a 90-day goal in reflection, and a 12-month goal just beginning intake. Progress is tracked per goal, not as a global "how are you doing."

Reflection without action is just processing.

Every session ends with a concrete assignment — an action step with a specific verb and a due date. Every commitment is public. Not performative — public. The act of making something visible to someone else is the mechanism that breaks the pattern.

The pattern is named, not treated.

This is not therapy. The coach doesn't try to resolve the underlying resistance. It names it precisely, in the context of the current goal, so the builder can see what they're doing. "The identity anchor pattern that slowed you on the consulting goal is appearing here too — you keep calling this a project instead of a business." That observation, made at the right moment, is more useful than six months of exploration.

03 — RESISTANCE PATTERNS

Six patterns. One per person.

Pattern The tell What it sounds like
Perfectionist It's not ready yet "I need to fix one more thing before I send it"
Imposter Who am I to do this? "People with real credentials would see through this"
Validator seeker Always checking "Let me run it by a few people first and see what they think"
Scope expander Can't stop adding "What if I also added the onboarding flow before launching"
Identity anchor Still the old thing "I'm not really a consultant, I come from operations"
Visibility avoider Hiding from the audience "I'll publish when it's more polished, when there's more to show"
The pattern is established in the person intake (once, ever) and remains stable. It manifests differently on each goal — but the underlying structure is the same.
04 — HOW IT WORKS

The portfolio model

The system coaches goals, not people. The person is profiled once — background, resistance pattern, dominant lens, behavioral signals. After that, each goal the person names moves through its own independent reflection-to-action loop.

PERSON (profiled once) ├── background + build ├── resistance pattern: identity_anchor ├── dominant lens: business └── goals[] ├── 30-day: "First external conversation" → commitment phase ├── 90-day: "First paid engagement" → reflection phase └── 12-month: "Full-time consultant" → intake phase

Each session focuses on one goal — the most recently touched active goal by default. The person can redirect to any goal. The coach picks up exactly where that goal's phase work left off.

Six phases. Each goal moves independently.

Intake same-session transition
Establish what this goal actually is. Five questions. Goal description confirmed, first action step assigned. Transitions to reflection in the same session.
Reflection gate: 2+ contradictions · 1 action step · 3 days
Surface the contradictions specific to this goal. What the builder says they want vs. what they actually do. One to two sessions. The coach is watching for the gap between stated intent and observable behavior.
Clarity gate: goal in one sentence · 1 action step · 3 days
Define what done looks like — not perfectly done, the version where the goal is honestly behind them. One sentence. Not a vision statement. A finish line.
Resistance gate: pattern named in context · card deployed · 1 action step · 3 days
Name how the person's pattern manifests on this specific goal. Deploy an Oblique Strategies card. The pattern has been named globally — here it is named in context, against this specific goal and the specific behavior it's producing right now.
Commitment gate: commitment declared · output package generated
One public act toward this goal. Specific, declared in session, assigned a ladder rung (1–7). The output package includes a share post, a print card, and a daily reminder sequence for 7 days.
Accountability gate: outcome recorded
Did they do it? What happened? Loop back to commitment or mark goal complete. Missed commitments are not erased — they are recorded with the pattern they represent.
All transitions except intake→reflection require a minimum of 3 days in the current phase AND content gates (completed action steps, specific things named). The system won't rush phases.
06 — PERSON INTAKE

One conversation. Permanent calibration.

The person intake runs once, at the beginning, before any goal work. It takes 20 minutes. It establishes:

Domain + transition
Background and transition type — deliberate vs. pushed into change.
Build + ship history
What's been built and what's actually been shipped. The gap matters.
Resistance pattern
The dominant pattern and its behavioral signals — avoidance language, engagement triggers.
Notification preferences
How and when the builder wants to receive daily commitment signals.
After the intake, the coach knows: the pattern to watch for, the language that signals the pattern is active, and what specifically tends to re-engage the person when motivation drops. This calibration is permanent and applies to every goal.

Goals are not set during person intake. The intake is about the person. Goals come immediately after.

07 — THE COACH'S VOICE

Direct. Specific. No generic encouragement.

Opens anchored, not open-ended.

The coach opens every session anchored to something concrete: the last action step, the active commitment, or where the phase work left off. Never "how are you?" or "what would you like to work on today?" The coach already knows.

Uses the pattern by name.

Not abstractly — "the identity anchor pattern that appeared in session 3 is appearing here again." The pattern is named every time it appears, in the context of the current goal and the specific behavior it's producing.

Asks, doesn't advise.

The coach does not give advice. It asks questions that assume the builder already knows the answer. The question is the intervention — not the content of a hypothetical answer the coach is steering toward.

Assigns, doesn't suggest.

Action steps are directives. "Before we talk again: [specific act]." Not "you might consider…" The verb is specific. The deadline is specific. There is no ambiguity about what was assigned.

Tone calibration.

Three modes — direct, structured, balanced — set during intake and applied throughout. The mode affects sentence length and question cadence, not the commitment to specificity.

Voice contrast

Generic
"That sounds really challenging. Have you thought about what might be holding you back?"
Unlabeled
"You said you'd send the bio by Friday. You didn't. What specifically stopped you in the moment you were about to do it?"

09 — COMMITMENT ARCHITECTURE

Every commitment is public. Every session ends with an action.

Action steps
Assigned every session. Due in 48 hours. Tagged to a specific goal and phase. They escalate through 5 levels of exercise intensity per resistance pattern. Level 1 might be "write three sentences, don't send them." Level 5 is direct public interaction.
Commitment ladder
Seven rungs from "write and keep private" to "direct pitch to a specific named person." Rungs escalate visibility. The coach assigns the appropriate rung based on phase and resistance pattern.
Daily signals
After a commitment is declared, the builder receives a daily reminder for 7 days. Each day's message is different — escalating from "what's your first sentence?" on day 1 to "did you post it? what happened in the first 24 hours?" on day 7.
Artifact requirement
The commitment is never just "think about it." It produces a shareable artifact: a specific post, a message to a specific person, a published piece of work.

Commitment ladder — 7 rungs

01
Write it. Keep it private. Show no one.
02
Share with one trusted person — not for feedback, for accountability.
03
Post to a private or limited audience (draft, group, close contacts).
04
Publish publicly with no direct notification to anyone specific.
05
Publish and send to one person who isn't in your immediate circle.
06
Direct outreach to a named person you don't already know.
07
Direct pitch — a specific offer, to a specific named person, for a specific outcome.

Alongside the main coaching loop, four standalone specialist agents are available for focused work. Each draws entirely from one book's philosophy. Each ends every response with a concrete action.

11 — THE TOOLS

Two tools. Both end with movement.

Clearness Committee

A digital adaptation of the Quaker-derived Clearness Committee process. The user brings a dilemma. The committee asks nine questions in three stages — three surface questions, three medium, three deep — with no advice given at any point.

Ends with two mirror observations: not judgments, not recommendations. Observations that reflect what was noticed in the language and energy of the answers.

Developed by the Quaker tradition, adapted for secular use by Parker J. Palmer.

The committee gives no advice. It only asks honest questions — questions where the asker genuinely cannot know the answer.
Task Simplifier

Takes one complex, vague, or overwhelming task and returns: a simplified statement of what the task actually is, ordered action steps starting with verbs, a first move executable in 30 minutes, and two potential blockers to watch for.

Built on Scott Belsky's execution framework from Making Ideas Happen.

12 — TRACKING

What gets tracked and why

Portfolio Board
Each active goal shown as a row with a mini 6-station phase rail. Phase progress, days elapsed, time-gate status. Active commitments and pending action steps visible at a glance.
Commitment history
Every commitment declared, its outcome (done / missed / partial), and the outcome notes. Missed commitments are not erased — they're recorded with the pattern they represent.
Publishing log
Every public act logged with URL, platform, date, and commitment reference.
Session records
Each session produces a structured record — goals discussed, resistance pattern active or not, action step assigned, commitment declared, safety state, session summary.
Pattern tracking
The pattern is named in every session note where it appeared. Over time the record shows exactly where and how the pattern manifests across goals.
Nothing is hidden, nothing is softened. The commitment history shows missed commitments. The pattern tracking shows where resistance appeared. The record is honest because the builder needs an honest mirror, not a curated highlight reel.
